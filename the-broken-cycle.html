The Broken Cycle

Every civilization that has ever existed has followed the same arc. Struggle produces strength. Strength produces prosperity. Prosperity produces comfort. Comfort produces weakness. And then — this is the part that matters — weakness produces crisis, and crisis produces struggle again. The wheel turns. The forest fire clears the deadwood. Ibn Khaldun saw it in the 14th century. Sir John Glubb mapped it across ten empires and three thousand years. Peter Turchin built mathematical models that predicted, years in advance, the American instability of 2020. The details vary. The structure never does.

The implicit promise in every version of this story is renewal. The cycle completes. The fever breaks. Something new grows. This essay argues that the renewal phase may no longer be available to us — not because we've transcended the cycle, but because we've accidentally broken the machinery that made it turn.

The Comfort Ratchet

The familiar part of the story is worth stating briefly because the unfamiliar part depends on it.

Every successful system is built under pressure. When consequences are real and immediate, competence is selected for ruthlessly. The early Roman Republic rewarded martial virtue because the alternative was annihilation. The founding team of any great company works with desperate intensity because the alternative is bankruptcy. These aren't choices. They're environmental necessities.

Success removes the necessity. The borders are secure. The surplus is large enough to absorb waste. And then a simple, mechanical process begins: every inefficiency that the system can afford to tolerate, it will tolerate. Not out of malice or stupidity, but because efficiency requires discomfort, and humans migrate toward comfort the moment the cost of doing so is not immediate. Parasitic coalitions accumulate — groups that extract value while contributing nothing, protected because the benefits of extraction are concentrated among the few who capture them while the costs are spread invisibly across everyone else. Each one is too small to trigger a response. In aggregate, they are the arterial plaque of civilizations.

Any system that successfully insulates its participants from the consequences of their decisions will accumulate decision-making pathologies until it either fails or re-establishes contact with consequences. This is not a theory. It is a law, and it operates at every scale — startups that get comfortable, militaries in peacetime, empires in their golden age. The only question has ever been: what happens when the accumulated pathologies reach critical mass?

What Used to Happen Next

What used to happen was a crisis. Not a recession — a genuine, system-threatening rupture. Civil war. Revolution. Conquest. State fiscal collapse that could not be papered over. The crisis was brutal but functional. It cleared the parasitic coalitions, pruned the deadwood, and reset selection pressure so that competence mattered again. The people and institutions that survived were, by definition, the ones adapted to reality rather than to comfort. And they rebuilt.

The crisis was the immune system. It was never pleasant. But it was always what came next, and it was what made "next" possible.

The Behavioral Shift

Here is where the story changes.

Every crisis in history — every revolution, every civilizational renewal — was driven by a specific demographic: large numbers of young, aggressive, risk-tolerant men with frustrated ambitions and nothing left to lose. Turchin calls them counter-elites. Ibn Khaldun called them the hard men of the periphery. Whatever the label, they shared a biological and behavioral profile: high testosterone, high aggression, high willingness to engage in direct, physical, high-stakes confrontation with the existing order.

That profile is disappearing, and it is disappearing for reasons that do not reverse on their own.

Testosterone levels in men have been declining at roughly 1% per year for at least four decades. This is not simply an aging effect — a 40-year-old man today has substantially less testosterone than a 40-year-old man in 1987. A 60-year-old in 2002 had lower levels than a 60-year-old fifteen years earlier. The decline persists after controlling for obesity, smoking, and physical activity. It appears in every industrialized population that has measured it. Israeli military data covering over 100,000 men confirmed the trend is continuing through at least 2019, with no sign of leveling off.

Endocrine-disrupting chemicals probably contribute. But the more fundamental cause is likely simpler and harder to fix: civilization itself. Testosterone is not a fixed quantity. It is a biological dial that responds to environment. It rises with competition, physical demand, danger, and real status contests. It falls with sedentary work, physical safety, conflict avoidance, and the absence of genuine stakes. A man whose daily existence involves sitting in climate-controlled rooms, navigating bureaucratic politics, and facing no physical challenge more serious than a commute is living in an environment that tells his endocrine system testosterone is unnecessary. And the system listens.

One percent per year is invisible in real time. Compounded over four decades, it is a population-level transformation. But the hormonal shift is only half the story — and not even the more important half. The other half is what happens when this biological change interacts with institutional culture.

As competitive, risk-tolerant behavior declines in a population, institutions don't just lose dynamism — they actively restructure themselves around different values. Selection within organizations shifts from competence under pressure to agreeableness under consensus. The qualities that get people hired, promoted, and celebrated shift from the ability to make hard calls and tolerate conflict toward the ability to manage feelings, avoid disruption, and maintain harmony. Risk is reframed as recklessness. Directness is reframed as hostility. The institutional culture feminizes — not as a metaphor, but as a literal shift in which behavioral traits are selected for, mirroring the biological shift in the population that staffs them.

This is the feedback loop that makes the process self-reinforcing across generations. Lower testosterone produces men who are less competitive and more conflict-averse. Those men build and reinforce institutional cultures that reward consensus over confrontation. Those institutional cultures further suppress the behaviors and environments that maintain testosterone. The next generation is shaped by institutions that are one more step removed from competitive reality. Each turn of the loop is imperceptible. The cumulative effect, over decades, is a civilization that has reorganized itself — biologically, culturally, and institutionally — around the avoidance of the very pressures that built it.

Previous civilizations in their decline phase had culturally softened elites. Roman patricians of the 5th century were decadent and politically feckless. But the underlying population was still biologically capable of violence, risk, and aggressive collective action. When material conditions became desperate enough, the capacity was there. The culture was soft, but the hardware was intact. We are running a novel experiment: what happens when the hardware shifts too? When the population's endocrine profile has been quietly recalibrated, generation by generation, to an environment of safety and comfort that their ancestors never experienced?

The men who stormed the Bastille, who fought at Gettysburg, who marched on Rome were not produced by the endocrine environment of the modern open-plan office. And the men produced by the modern open-plan office are not, in aggregate, the men who produce revolutions. This does not mean individual modern men are incapable of courage. It means the statistical distribution has shifted. The fat tail of high-aggression, high-risk-tolerance young men that every historical crisis drew upon is thinner than it has ever been, and getting thinner, driven by a self-reinforcing loop between biology and culture that has no obvious exit.

The Monoculture Problem

But even if internal renewal is breaking down, there has always been a backup mechanism. Historically, civilizations didn't need to save themselves. They got replaced. Rome rotted, and the Germanic tribes swept in. The Song Dynasty stagnated, and the Mongols arrived. One society's comfort phase was another's struggle phase. There was always someone hungrier, harder, and more adapted to reality waiting in the wings. The cycle worked not just within civilizations but between them, and this redundancy is what kept the overall trajectory of the species advancing even when individual societies failed.

This backup no longer exists, and Robin Hanson has articulated why.

Hanson's argument centers on what he calls cultural drift — the observation that as global communication, trade, and institutional convergence accelerate, the world's distinct cultures are collapsing into a single operating system. Not at the surface level — we still enjoy different cuisines, holidays, and aesthetics. Hanson calls this "boutique multiculturalism." The convergence that matters runs deeper: fertility norms, gender roles, attitudes toward risk and competition, institutional structures, credentialism, what counts as prestigious. A university-educated professional in Seoul, São Paulo, Nairobi, and Stockholm lives functionally the same life, holds functionally the same values, and is making functionally the same reproductive choices. The surface diversity masks a profound and accelerating homogeneity in everything that actually drives civilizational fitness.

This means the historical replacement mechanism has lost its supply chain. India's fertility rate has already fallen below replacement. East Asia's has cratered. Sub-Saharan Africa, the last region with high fertility, is modernizing along the same trajectory on a time delay. These are not alternative civilizations incubating in the wings. They are earlier points on the same curve, converging on the same behavioral and demographic profile, subject to the same comfort ratchet once they arrive.

For most of human history, civilizational decline was a local event. Humanity maintained a portfolio of societies at different stages, and the overall trajectory advanced because someone was always in the growth phase. We have collapsed that portfolio into a single position. If it declines, there is no hedge. No outside. No hungry periphery. No hard men waiting.

This is the full shape of the trap. Internal renewal is breaking because the biological and behavioral capacity for crisis is being dampened by the ordinary operation of comfort. External renewal is breaking because there is no civilizationally distinct outside left to provide competitive pressure or replacement. The cycle had two independent paths to renewal, and we are closing both simultaneously.

Japan

If the thesis sounds abstract, Japan makes it concrete.

By every structural indicator Turchin tracks, Japan should have experienced a major political crisis in the past two decades. It has the highest government debt-to-GDP ratio in the developed world. It has overproduced credentialed graduates for thirty years running. Real wages have stagnated for a generation. These are the exact preconditions — elite overproduction, popular immiseration, fiscal stress — that preceded acute crises in every previous case Turchin studied.

Japan's postwar violence cycle had fired on schedule: massive protests and domestic terrorism in the 1960s and early 1970s, driven by a young, large, frustrated population with recent memory of genuine hardship. The next expected firing simply didn't happen. Every structural pressure was present. The behavioral response was not.

Instead, Japan got hikikomori — over a million young men withdrawing into their bedrooms. Birth rates collapsed to levels that will halve the population within a few generations. Political engagement flatlined. The country entered a holding pattern of quiet, managed decline: not prosperous enough to call successful, not catastrophic enough to force change. Three lost decades and counting. No revolution. No renewal. No crisis. Just drift.

Japan is not an anomaly. It is a preview. South Korea, with a fertility rate below 0.7, may be further along. Western Europe is on the same trajectory. The United States is partially buffered by immigration — which imports populations that haven't yet completed the process — but native-born trends point the same direction.

The wheel was supposed to turn. It didn't. The population that was supposed to revolt was too old, too few, too hormonally recalibrated, and too digitally pacified to produce anything beyond anonymous frustration online.

What the End of the Cycle Looks Like

It does not look like collapse. Collapse requires energy. What the broken cycle produces is quieter — a society too comfortable to thrive but too stable to fail. Too medicated to revolt but too stagnant to build. A long, slow deflation of vitality, masked by technological sophistication and material abundance, visible only in the statistics that nobody wants to discuss at dinner: declining birth rates, declining testosterone, declining social trust, declining institutional legitimacy, declining willingness to take any consequential risk.

The scoreboard still shows a lead. The institutions still stand. The GDP ticks upward. But the lead was built by people who are dead or retired, and the institutions are running on the momentum of their design rather than the quality of their current operators. The gap between what the civilization has and what it is widens every year — invisible until the inherited capital is finally tested and found insufficient, with no one remaining who has the capacity or will to rebuild.

Civilizations that failed through crisis lost much, but they retained something essential: the capacity for renewal. What the broken cycle threatens is different. Not a dramatic fall but an asymptotic approach toward irrelevance. A species that solved every problem except the one created by solving all the others.

The Wildcard

There is one variable no historical model accounts for, because it has never existed: artificial intelligence.

If the argument of this essay holds — if the human machinery of renewal is broken both internally and externally — then AI is the only candidate for a force that could reimpose selection pressure on institutions and populations that have lost the ability to generate it themselves. Not as a policy tool or a productivity enhancer, but as something more fundamental: an external source of competitive reality-contact in a world that has otherwise eliminated it.

Whether it will function this way is a different question. AI is being built by people who are thoroughly embedded in the institutional culture this essay describes — the same consensus-oriented, risk-averse, safety-maximizing culture that is both symptom and accelerant of the decline. The default trajectory is not disruption. It is absorption. AI gets folded into the existing comfort apparatus the same way every previous technology has been: first as a tool of institutional control, then as a sedative, optimized for engagement and agreeableness rather than truth and challenge.

The hopeful case — that AI becomes a force that holds institutions and individuals accountable to reality regardless of their preference for comfort — requires swimming against every incentive currently operating. It is possible. It is not the default. And the window in which the architecture gets decided may be shorter than the people making those decisions understand.

If that window closes in the wrong direction, AI becomes the final and most effective layer of insulation between humanity and consequences — the technology that makes the decline comfortable enough that no one ever has sufficient reason to reverse it.

Coda

Civilization is the project of removing humans from brutal contact with reality. This is its purpose and its highest achievement. It is also, if this essay is right, its terminal diagnosis.

The cycle used to save us. Decay would reach a breaking point, the crisis would come, and contact with reality would be restored — violently, painfully, but effectively. We could afford to be careless because the system had a reset.

We may have broken the reset. Not through malice or negligence, but by being extraordinarily good at the one thing civilization was always trying to do. We broke it internally, by producing populations whose biology and culture are calibrated to comfort rather than crisis. We broke it externally, by converging the entire world onto a single cultural trajectory with no outside left to supply renewal.

The question now is whether a species that has spent ten thousand years building a wall between itself and the consequences of its own decisions can find a way to thrive behind it — or whether the wall was always the thing that would, slowly and comfortably, entomb it.
